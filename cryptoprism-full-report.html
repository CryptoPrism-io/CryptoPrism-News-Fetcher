Chapter 1

Executive Summary

CryptoPrism is a fully automated system that reads crypto news, engineers features from price data, and produces daily buy/hold/sell predictions for 1,000 cryptocurrencies. It runs entirely on GitHub Actions cloud infrastructure, requiring zero human intervention. The predictions feed directly into a PostgreSQL database that trading bots query in real time.

Over seven months of development, the system evolved from a simple news scraper into a multi-model ensemble that achieves an Information Coefficient (IC) of 0.129 on out-of-sample test data — exceeding our 0.10 target. This means the model's daily predictions have a meaningful, consistent correlation with actual future returns. In quantitative finance, an IC above 0.05 is considered useful; above 0.10 is excellent.

Chapter 2

Project Genesis & Timeline

Cryptocurrency markets are unlike any other asset class. They trade 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. A single tweet from a prominent figure can move prices 10% in minutes. News from regulatory bodies, exchange hacks, protocol upgrades, and partnership announcements create a constant stream of price-moving information that no human trader can fully process.

Traditional stock markets have closing bells, earnings seasons, and established patterns. Crypto has none of that. Correlations between coins shift rapidly — Bitcoin's dominance means most altcoins move in lockstep during panic events, yet diverge significantly during altcoin seasons. This creates both a problem and an opportunity: the problem is that raw price data alone is insufficient for prediction; the opportunity is that systematic processing of news and cross-coin signals can provide an edge.

CryptoPrism was born from a simple observation: no freely available system existed that combined crypto news sentiment analysis with quantitative feature engineering at scale. Commercial products existed at $500+/month, but they were black boxes with no transparency into their methodology. We set out to build an open, auditable alternative.

The system has accumulated over 66,000 articles since its inception. The growth reflects both the increasing number of sources we integrated and the natural growth of crypto news coverage during the 2025-2026 bull market cycle.

Why This Matters

Most crypto traders rely on gut feeling, social media hype, or simple technical indicators. These approaches fail at scale because they cannot process the volume of information that moves crypto markets. CryptoPrism processes all of it — 1,400 articles per day, price data for 1,000 coins, cross-coin relationships, and market regime signals — and distils it into a single actionable signal per coin, per day.

The system is designed with a clear separation of concerns: data collection is isolated from feature engineering, which is isolated from model training, which is isolated from inference. Each component can be upgraded independently without affecting the others. This modularity is what allowed us to iterate rapidly from a broken first model to a production-quality ensemble in just two months.

Chapter 3

Data Collection Pipeline

The foundation of any ML system is its data. CryptoPrism collects news articles from the CryptoCompare API, one of the largest aggregators of cryptocurrency news. The API provides access to articles from 33 distinct sources, ranging from established financial outlets like CoinDesk and CoinTelegraph to crypto-native publications and industry blogs.

Our collection pipeline runs hourly via GitHub Actions. Each run fetches the most recent articles, deduplicates them against existing records using the article URL as a unique constraint, and inserts new articles into the cc_news table. This append-only architecture ensures we never lose data and never create duplicates.

Source Quality Tiers

Not all news sources are created equal. We classify our 33 sources into three tiers based on their editorial standards, publication frequency, and market impact:

Tier 1 — High Impact

Premium Sources

CoinDesk, CoinTelegraph, The Block, Decrypt, Bloomberg Crypto. These outlets break major stories and have the highest correlation with subsequent price movements. They represent approximately 25% of our article volume but drive the majority of sentiment signals.

Tier 2 — Standard

Reliable Coverage

BeInCrypto, CryptoSlate, NewsBTC, Bitcoinist, AMBCrypto. These sources provide solid daily coverage with good breadth across coins. They form the backbone of our volume, contributing about 45% of articles.

Tier 3 — Supplementary

Niche & Regional

Smaller publications, DeFi-focused outlets, and regional sources. Lower individual impact but valuable for breadth — they often cover smaller altcoins that Tier 1 sources ignore. About 30% of volume.

Sentiment Distribution

After FinBERT processes each article, we classify sentiment as positive, neutral, or negative. The overall distribution reveals a positive skew — which is expected in crypto markets where promotional content is common. This is why we use relative sentiment (changes from baseline) rather than absolute sentiment as a signal.

The Three Databases

CryptoPrism uses a three-database architecture on Google Cloud PostgreSQL. Each database serves a distinct purpose, and understanding this architecture is critical because a misconfiguration between them caused our most significant bug — one that rendered the model effectively blind.

The "Blind Training" Bug

Critical bug discovered in April 2026

The model was training on dbcp instead of cp_backtest. Since dbcp only stores the latest 1-day snapshot of each FE table, the model could only see 1 out of 54 features across time. It was like trying to forecast weather by looking at a single thermometer reading instead of years of historical data. The fix was a single environment variable: DB_BACKTEST_NAME=cp_backtest. After switching, IC jumped from -0.007 to +0.081 overnight.

This bug is instructive because it was invisible. The code ran without errors, the model trained without warnings, and it even produced plausible-looking predictions. Only when we audited the actual row counts being fed to the model did we discover the discrepancy: instead of 2 million training rows, the model was seeing fewer than 3,000.

Chapter 5

Feature Engineering Deep Dive

Feature engineering is the art of transforming raw data into signals that a machine learning model can learn from. Think of it this way: if you wanted to predict whether a student would pass an exam, you could give the model their raw study hours — or you could give it more useful features like "hours studied in the last week", "average grade improvement trend", and "hours studied relative to class average". The latter tells the model more about what actually matters.

In CryptoPrism, raw data means daily open/high/low/close/volume (OHLCV) prices for 1,000 coins. Feature engineering transforms this into 50 carefully constructed signals spread across 10 tables, each capturing a different aspect of market behaviour.

Feature Count by Table Family

50 features across 10 FE table families

Detailed Feature Table Descriptions

1. FE_PCT_CHANGE 5 features

The most fundamental signals: how much did the price change? These capture daily returns, cumulative performance, and risk characteristics.

d_pct_1d (daily return), d_pct_cum_ret (cumulative return over window), d_pct_var (variance / volatility), d_pct_cvar (conditional VaR / tail risk), d_pct_max_dd (maximum drawdown)

These five features alone account for the top 3 SHAP importances in the model. They answer the question: "Is this coin volatile, trending, or crashing?"

2. FE_MOMENTUM_SIGNALS 5 features

Momentum indicators measure the speed and strength of price movement. A coin rising slowly and steadily looks very different from one that just spiked 20%.

m_mom_roc_bin (Rate of Change binary), m_mom_willr_bin (Williams %R), m_mom_smi_bin (Stochastic Momentum), m_mom_cmo_bin (Chande Momentum), m_pct_1d (1-day momentum percentage)

Binary signals (_bin) indicate whether the indicator is above or below its threshold. This discretisation helps the tree model find clear decision boundaries.

3. FE_OSCILLATORS_SIGNALS 6 features

Oscillators detect overbought and oversold conditions. When a coin has risen too fast, oscillators signal that a reversal may be coming — and vice versa.

m_osc_macd_bin (MACD cross), m_osc_cci_bin (Commodity Channel Index), m_osc_adx_bin (Average Directional Index), m_osc_stoch_bin (Stochastic), m_osc_rsi_bin (Relative Strength Index), m_osc_bb_bin (Bollinger Band position)

MACD and RSI are two of the most widely used indicators in all of finance. Including their signals ensures the model captures patterns that human traders also watch.

4. FE_TVV_SIGNALS 6 features

Trading Volume and Value signals track money flow. Price movement without volume is unreliable; these features tell the model whether volume confirms the price trend.

m_tvv_obv (On-Balance Volume), m_tvv_sma_cross (SMA crossover), m_tvv_ema_cross (EMA crossover), m_tvv_cmf (Chaikin Money Flow), m_tvv_vol_zscore (volume z-score), m_tvv_vol_sma_ratio (volume/SMA ratio)

Chaikin Money Flow (CMF) is particularly valuable — it measures buying vs selling pressure by looking at where the close falls within the day's range, weighted by volume.

5. FE_RATIOS_SIGNALS 11 features

The largest feature table. These are risk-adjusted performance metrics borrowed from portfolio management theory. They answer: "Is this coin delivering good returns for the risk taken?"

d_rat_alpha_bin (excess return vs BTC), d_rat_beta_bin (BTC sensitivity), d_rat_sharpe_bin (Sharpe ratio), d_rat_sortino_bin (downside-risk-adjusted), d_rat_treynor_bin (systematic-risk-adjusted), d_rat_info_bin (information ratio), d_rat_calmar_bin (return/drawdown), d_rat_win_rate (% positive days), d_rat_payoff (avg win / avg loss), d_rat_profit_factor (gross profit / gross loss), v_rat_sharpe_bin (volume-adjusted Sharpe)

Alpha and Beta are directly interpretable: high alpha means the coin outperforms Bitcoin on a risk-adjusted basis; high beta means it amplifies Bitcoin's movements.

6. FE_FEAR_GREED_CMC 1 feature

A single but powerful market-wide sentiment indicator. The Fear and Greed Index ranges from 0 (extreme fear) to 100 (extreme greed) and captures crowd psychology.

fear_greed_index (0-100 scale, daily)

This is a cross-sectional feature — it has the same value for every coin on a given day. It acts as a regime indicator, helping the model condition its predictions on broad market mood.

7. FE_NEWS_SIGNALS 12 features

The output of our NLP pipeline. FinBERT sentiment scores are aggregated per coin per day and transformed into multiple signal types.

sentiment_score_1d, sentiment_score_3d, sentiment_score_7d (rolling averages), sentiment_std_7d (sentiment volatility), article_count_1d, article_count_7d, sentiment_momentum (change in sentiment), coverage_rank (relative coverage), positive_ratio_7d, negative_ratio_7d, event_flag_exchange, event_flag_regulatory

Rolling averages at 1, 3, and 7-day windows capture both sudden sentiment spikes and persistent narrative shifts. The event flags detect specific high-impact categories.

8. FE_BTC_RESIDUALS 4 features

Stripping away Bitcoin's influence to reveal each coin's independent behaviour. Since 70-90% of altcoin returns are explained by Bitcoin, the residual is where the real alpha lives.

btc_beta (rolling 30-day BTC sensitivity), btc_residual_1d (today's residual), btc_residual_7d (7-day cumulative residual), residual_vol_ratio (residual volatility)

See Chapter 7 for the full mathematical derivation and visualisation of this decomposition.

9. FE_RESIDUAL_FEATURES 8 features

Second-order signals extracted from the residuals themselves. These are the output of Workstream 6 (WS6) — the first workstream completed in our IC improvement plan.

res_momentum_3d, res_momentum_7d, res_momentum_14d (residual trend), res_zscore_30d (statistical extremity), res_vol_regime (volatility regime ratio), res_autocorr_7d, res_autocorr_14d (serial correlation), res_volume_interaction (residual x volume)

See Chapter 13 for detailed explanation. These features contributed +0.005 to IC.

10. FE_CROSS_COIN 8 features

Cross-sectional rank features that place each coin in context of the entire market. An absolute return of +3% means nothing without knowing that the market average was +5%.

cc_ret_rank_1d, cc_ret_rank_7d (return percentile rank), cc_vol_rank_1d (volume percentile rank), cc_mktcap_momentum (market cap rank change), cc_breadth_20d (% above 20-day SMA), cc_advance_decline (advancing / declining ratio), cc_dispersion (cross-sectional return std), cc_hhi_volume (volume concentration)

See Chapter 14 for details. These features were the single largest IC contributor (+0.043) and, critically, collapsed the val/test generalisation gap.

Chapter 6

The Sentiment Engine — FinBERT

FinBERT is a variant of Google's BERT language model that has been specifically fine-tuned on financial text. Unlike general-purpose sentiment tools (like VADER, which counts positive and negative words), FinBERT understands financial context. It knows that "the stock tanked" is negative, that "breaking resistance" is positive, and that "the Fed raised rates" requires nuance.

Why FinBERT Over Alternatives?

Chosen

FinBERT

Pre-trained on Reuters TRC2, financial web pages, and SEC filings. Free, runs locally on GPU. Processes 256 articles per batch. Standard in academic finance NLP research. Consistent, deterministic outputs. Cost: $0 (runs on our RTX 3070).

Rejected: GPT-4

Large Language Models

Higher accuracy on some benchmarks but costs $0.03-0.06 per article. At 66K articles that is $2,000-$4,000 per full backfill. Non-deterministic outputs (temperature variability). API rate limits would bottleneck processing. Overkill for binary/ternary classification.

Rejected: VADER

Rule-Based Sentiment

Dictionary-based approach: counts positive and negative words. Fast but shallow. Cannot understand negation ("not bullish"), sarcasm, or domain-specific language. Consistently underperforms transformer models on financial text benchmarks.

Rejected: Generic BERT

Base BERT Model

Pre-trained on Wikipedia and BookCorpus. Understands language structure but lacks financial domain knowledge. Would require additional fine-tuning on financial data, which is exactly what FinBERT already provides out of the box.

How FinBERT Works

FinBERT architecture: from article text to sentiment score

The process is straightforward: each article's title and body text are concatenated, tokenized into a sequence of up to 512 tokens (sub-word units), and fed through 12 transformer layers. The [CLS] token's final representation — a 768-dimensional vector that encodes the article's overall meaning — passes through a linear classification head that outputs three probabilities: positive, neutral, and negative. The final sentiment score is calculated as P(positive) - P(negative), giving a value between -1 and +1.

From Articles to Signals

Individual article scores are noisy. A single article might be about a partnership announcement but get classified as neutral because of cautious language. To extract reliable signals, we aggregate scores at the coin-day level and apply rolling windows:

  1. Per-article score: FinBERT produces P(pos) - P(neg) for each article
  2. Coin mapping: Each article is mapped to one or more coins via our category mapper
  3. Daily aggregation: Average sentiment score per coin per day
  4. Rolling averages: 1-day, 3-day, and 7-day rolling means smooth out noise
  5. Signal derivation: Momentum (change in sentiment), volatility (std), coverage rank

Chapter 7

BTC Residual Decomposition

The single biggest challenge in crypto prediction is Bitcoin dominance. When Bitcoin rises 5%, most altcoins rise too — some by 3%, others by 8%. When Bitcoin crashes, nearly everything crashes. This means that 70-90% of any altcoin's daily return is simply "following Bitcoin". If you only look at raw returns, you are mostly predicting Bitcoin, not the individual coin.

BTC Residual Decomposition solves this by mathematically separating each coin's return into two components: the part explained by Bitcoin (the "beta component") and the part that is independent (the "residual" or "alpha component"). This is a direct application of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) from traditional finance, adapted for crypto.

The Mathematics

For each coin, we run a rolling 30-day ordinary least squares (OLS) regression:

Regression formula

coin_return = alpha + beta x BTC_return + residual

Where alpha is the coin's average daily excess return independent of Bitcoin, beta measures how much the coin amplifies Bitcoin's movements, and residual is today's unexplained component — the part of the return that cannot be attributed to Bitcoin.

Visual Decomposition

Decomposing a coin's total return into BTC component + residual

What Beta Tells Us

Beta is a measure of market sensitivity. In crypto, it tells you how aggressively a coin reacts to Bitcoin's movements:

Beta < 0.5

Low Sensitivity

Stablecoins, some DeFi tokens. Barely reacts to BTC. Independent price drivers (usage, governance, staking yields).

Beta = 0.8 - 1.2

Market-Tracking

Most large-cap altcoins (ETH, SOL, ADA). Moves roughly in line with Bitcoin. The majority of coins fall in this range.

Beta > 1.5

High Leverage

Small-cap, volatile coins. Amplifies Bitcoin moves: if BTC rises 5%, these might rise 8-10%. Conversely, they crash harder in downturns.

Chapter 8

Model 1 — LightGBM

LightGBM (Light Gradient Boosting Machine) is the workhorse of CryptoPrism's prediction engine. Developed by Microsoft Research, it is the industry standard for tabular prediction tasks in finance. Nearly every top-performing solution in Kaggle competitions involving structured data uses some form of gradient-boosted trees, and LightGBM is the fastest and most memory-efficient implementation.

Why LightGBM?

Strength 1

Handles Missing Values Natively

Many of our features are sparse — a coin might not have any news coverage for days, or might be too new for 30-day rolling windows. LightGBM handles NaN values internally, learning which direction to send missing data at each split. No imputation needed.

Strength 2

Blazing Fast Training

500 trees train in under 30 seconds on our dataset. This enables rapid experimentation with hyperparameters, feature sets, and training windows. We can test a hypothesis and see results in minutes, not hours.

Strength 3

Mixed Feature Types

Our features include continuous values (sentiment scores, returns), binary signals (overbought/oversold flags), and ordinal values (ranks). LightGBM handles all of these without normalisation or encoding.

Strength 4

Interpretable via SHAP

Unlike neural networks, tree models can be explained. SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values tell us exactly which features drive each prediction and by how much. This is critical for debugging and building trust.

How Gradient-Boosted Trees Work

Imagine you are trying to predict tomorrow's return for a coin. The first tree makes a rough guess based on the most important feature (say, volatility). It gets some predictions right and some wrong. The second tree looks at the errors of the first tree and tries to correct them. The third tree corrects the second tree's errors. And so on, for 500 sequential trees.

Sequential error correction: how 500 trees build a prediction

Walk-Forward Training

Time series data requires special handling. You cannot randomly split data into train/test sets because that would allow future information to leak into training. Instead, we use walk-forward validation: train on the past, validate on the recent past, and test on the most recent data.

Walk-forward split: never peek into the future

Model 2 — LSTM Feature Extractor

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a type of recurrent neural network designed to learn sequential patterns. While LightGBM sees each day as an independent row of features, LSTM processes a 30-day sequence and learns how features evolve over time. It can detect patterns like "momentum has been building for two weeks" or "volatility spiked then settled" — temporal patterns that a tree model would miss.

Why LSTM?

Tree models like LightGBM are excellent at finding feature thresholds and interactions on a given day. But they are blind to the trajectory of features over time. An LSTM naturally captures concepts like:

  • Momentum persistence: Is a trend accelerating or decelerating?
  • Mean reversion patterns: Has a coin been overbought for too many days?
  • Volume confirmation: Is rising price accompanied by rising volume over time?
  • Sentiment shifts: Is sentiment gradually turning negative over a week?

Chapter 10

Model 3 — TCN Hourly Microstructure

Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) are an alternative to LSTMs for sequence modelling that use convolutions instead of recurrence. Where LSTMs process sequences one timestep at a time (slow, sequential), TCNs apply convolutions across the entire sequence in parallel (fast, GPU-friendly). For our hourly data (168 hours = 7 days), this parallelism matters.

Why TCN Over LSTM for Hourly Data?

Parallelisable

Faster Training

Convolutional operations run in parallel on GPU, unlike LSTM's sequential gate updates. With 684K hourly sequences, this speed advantage is significant — training completes in reasonable time.

Dilated Convolutions

Long-Range Vision

By increasing the dilation factor exponentially (1, 2, 4, 8), the network sees increasingly distant timesteps without needing deep stacking. Effective receptive field: ~45 hours from just 4 blocks.

Causal Padding

No Data Leakage

Causal convolutions ensure the output at time T only depends on inputs at time T and earlier. The network physically cannot peek into the future, a guarantee that LSTMs provide by design but standard CNNs do not.

Residual Connections

Stable Deep Networks

Each block adds its input directly to its output (skip connection), preventing gradient vanishing in deeper architectures. This allows us to stack 4 blocks without training instability.

Model 4 — HMM Regime Detector

Markets do not behave the same way all the time. During bull runs, momentum strategies work well — buy what is going up. During crashes, mean-reversion works better — buy what has fallen too far. During choppy sideways markets, neither works reliably. A model that does not know which regime it is in will be confused, applying bull-market logic during a crash.

The Hidden Markov Model (HMM) solves this by identifying the current market "state" from observable features. It is "hidden" because we do not directly observe the regime — we infer it from data like volatility, breadth, and momentum.

Known limitation

Chapter 12

The Ensemble — Putting It All Together

No single model captures all the patterns in financial data. LightGBM excels at finding feature thresholds. LSTM captures temporal sequences. TCN detects hourly microstructure. HMM identifies market regimes. FinBERT quantifies news sentiment. Each model sees a different facet of the same underlying reality. An ensemble combines them all, producing predictions that are more accurate and more stable than any individual component.

Why Ensemble Beats Individual Models

The mathematical intuition is simple: if two models make uncorrelated errors, combining them reduces the overall error. When the LSTM predicts "buy" but the LightGBM predicts "sell" because it sees a risk signal the LSTM missed, the ensemble can weigh both perspectives. In practice, ensemble methods consistently outperform individual models in machine learning competitions and in production financial systems.

Chapter 13

WS6 — Second-Order Residual Features

Workstream 6 was the first workstream completed in our IC improvement plan. The core idea: raw residuals (from BTC decomposition) are noisy. But extracting structure from those residuals — their trend, their volatility regime, their serial correlation — reveals hidden patterns that the model can learn from.

Think of it this way: if the residual itself is the weather today, second-order features are the weather patterns — "has it been getting progressively warmer?" or "is today's temperature unusually high compared to the last month?"

The 8 Features

res_momentum_3d / 7d / 14d

Rolling mean of residuals over 3, 7, and 14 days. Captures whether the coin is persistently outperforming (positive momentum) or underperforming (negative momentum) relative to what Bitcoin alone would predict. A rising 14-day residual momentum suggests a sustained coin-specific catalyst.

res_zscore_30d

How many standard deviations today's residual is from the 30-day mean. A z-score of +2 means the coin is outperforming at an unusually extreme level. Extreme z-scores often precede mean reversion — the coin has "stretched" too far from its normal behaviour.

res_vol_regime

The ratio of 7-day residual volatility to 30-day residual volatility. When this ratio is above 1.0, short-term volatility is expanding (something is happening to this coin specifically). When below 1.0, the coin is settling into a calmer regime. The model uses this to adjust its confidence in momentum signals.

res_autocorr_7d / 14d

Serial correlation of residuals over 7 and 14 day windows. High positive autocorrelation means "what happened to this coin's residual yesterday is likely to continue today" — a trending signal. Negative autocorrelation means mean-reversion is dominant. The model uses this to decide whether to chase or fade a move.

res_volume_interaction

The product of the residual and volume z-score. A large positive residual accompanied by high volume is much more meaningful than one on thin volume. This interaction term captures volume confirmation of residual moves.

Chapter 14

WS3 — Cross-Coin Features

Cross-sectional features place each coin in context of the entire market. A coin returning +3% today sounds positive — but if the market average return is +5%, that coin is actually underperforming. Absolute values are meaningless without relative context.

This is a well-known principle in quantitative finance: cross-sectional rank features are among the most robust predictors because they are naturally normalised, resistant to outliers, and capture relative positioning rather than absolute levels.

The 8 Features

cc_ret_rank_1d / 7d

Return Percentile Rank

Where does this coin rank in today's/this week's return distribution? A value of 0.95 means the coin is in the top 5% of performers. The 1d version captures daily winners/losers; the 7d version captures weekly trend leaders.

cc_vol_rank_1d

Volume Percentile Rank

Relative volume compared to other coins. High volume rank + high return rank = strong conviction move. High volume rank + low return rank = potential distribution (smart money selling into retail buying).

cc_mktcap_momentum

Market Cap Rank Change

Change in market-cap rank over the past 7 days. A coin climbing from rank 50 to rank 35 is gaining mindshare and capital inflow. Rapid rank changes often precede continued momentum.

cc_breadth_20d

Market Breadth

Percentage of coins above their 20-day simple moving average. When breadth is high (>70%), the rally is broad and healthy. When breadth diverges from price (price rising but breadth falling), a reversal may be imminent.

cc_advance_decline

Advance/Decline Ratio

Ratio of coins with positive returns to coins with negative returns. A value above 1.0 means more coins are rising than falling. This is a daily market sentiment gauge that complements the Fear and Greed Index.

cc_dispersion

Cross-Sectional Dispersion

Standard deviation of returns across all coins. High dispersion means coins are moving independently (good for stock picking). Low dispersion means everything moves together (hard to find alpha, macro-driven market).

cc_hhi_volume

Volume Concentration (HHI)

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index of volume across coins. High HHI means volume is concentrated in a few coins (Bitcoin dominance). Low HHI means volume is spread broadly (altcoin season). This is a structural market regime feature.

Results — The IC Journey

The Information Coefficient (IC) is the standard metric for evaluating cross-sectional prediction models in quantitative finance. It measures the rank correlation between the model's predicted signal and the actual future return. An IC of 0 means the model is no better than random; an IC of 1 would mean perfect prediction. In practice, ICs above 0.05 are considered useful, and above 0.10 are excellent.

The Stabilisation Story

The most interesting finding was not the IC level but the stabilisation pattern. After adding WS6 alone (42 features), the model's test IC was +0.229 (excellent) but its validation IC was -0.007 (terrible). This 0.236 gap screamed "overfitting" — the model was memorising test-period-specific patterns rather than learning generalisable relationships.

Adding WS3 cross-coin features (50 features) collapsed this gap to just 0.023. The key insight: cross-sectional rank features are inherently normalised. By ranking coins against each other rather than using raw values, the model becomes less sensitive to the specific distribution of returns in any given period. This is why cross-sectional models dominate quantitative equity investing.

What IC = 0.129 Means in Practice

Plain English interpretation

If you rank 1,000 coins by the model's prediction and split them into 5 equal groups (quintiles), the top quintile (most bullish predictions) will outperform the bottom quintile (most bearish predictions) by a meaningful margin on most days. Specifically, IC = 0.129 implies that on roughly 56% of days, the top quintile outperforms the bottom quintile. Over hundreds of days, this small edge compounds into significant alpha.

Walk-Forward Retraining (Sundays)

Every Sunday at 04:00 UTC, the model retrains with all data accumulated since the last training. The walk-forward window slides forward by one week, incorporating the latest market conditions. This ensures the model adapts to changing market dynamics while maintaining enough historical data for robust learning.

Each retraining run takes approximately 15 minutes on GitHub Actions. The new model is validated against the most recent 21 days, and metrics are logged to ML_MODEL_REGISTRY. If the new model's validation IC falls below 0.03, an alert is triggered and the previous model remains in production. This safety check prevents deploying a degraded model.

Trading Bot Integration Roadmap

The ultimate goal of CryptoPrism is to feed real-time signals into automated trading bots. The ML_SIGNALS table is already structured for bot consumption: each row contains a coin slug, date, and signal score (-1 to +1). The integration roadmap defines how trading bots will progressively incorporate these signals.

Five-Phase Integration Plan

Phased integration timeline

Phase Details

Phase 1: Hard Veto + Leverage Multiplier (~3h)

Conservative Start

The simplest integration: if the ML signal for a coin is strongly negative (below -0.5), the trading bot will not open any long positions on that coin. If the signal is strongly positive (above +0.5), the bot's position size can increase by 1.5x. This acts as a safety rail — the ML model can prevent bad trades but cannot independently initiate trades.

Phase 2: Take Profit Expansion + Volume Spike (~4h)

Profit Enhancement

When the ML signal aligns with the bot's existing position AND article volume for that coin is elevated, the take-profit target widens. The logic: if both price action AND news sentiment support the trade, the move likely has further to run. Volume spikes in news coverage amplify this conviction signal.

Phase 3: Full Signal Fusion — 70/30 (~6h)

Weighted Combination

The bot's existing price-based entry signal is combined with the ML signal: 70% weight on price action (the bot's proven strategy), 30% weight on ML predictions. The combined score must exceed a threshold for entry. This is the point where the ML model starts directly influencing trade decisions, not just filtering them.

Phase 4: LIVE Bot Integration (~3h)

Production Deployment

Connect to Binance via the live trading bot. ML_SIGNALS are read directly from the database at trade decision time. Position sizing, entry, and exit are all influenced by the ML score. Paper trading period first (2 weeks minimum), then gradual capital allocation.

Phase 5: Regime Filter (Future)

Adaptive Strategy

Once the HMM regime detector is improved (WS4), the bot will switch between strategies based on the current market regime. Momentum strategies during risk_on, mean-reversion during choppy, defensive positioning during risk_off. This is the most sophisticated integration level.

Chapter 18

Lessons Learned & What's Next

Seven months of building CryptoPrism has produced not just a working ML pipeline, but a set of hard-won insights about building quantitative systems. These lessons apply far beyond crypto prediction.

Six Key Lessons

Lesson 1

Infrastructure Bugs Are Silent Killers

The dual-database bug produced no errors, no warnings, and plausible-looking outputs. Only a manual row-count audit revealed that the model was training on 1/1000th of the intended data. Always verify your data pipeline end-to-end before trusting model metrics.

Lesson 2

Cross-Sectional Features Are Essential

Absolute feature values are inherently non-stationary — a 5% return means different things in different market conditions. Ranking features cross-sectionally (WS3) provided the largest single IC improvement and stabilised generalisation.

Lesson 3

Val/Test Gap Matters More Than Val IC

A model with val IC = 0.200 and test IC = 0.020 is useless in production. A model with val IC = 0.129 and test IC = 0.106 (gap = 0.023) is reliable. Always evaluate stability, not just peak performance. The gap tells you if the model generalises.

Lesson 4

GPU Matters for NLP at Scale

Processing 66K articles with FinBERT takes 6 hours on CPU but 12 minutes on GPU. This 30x speedup changed NLP from "run overnight" to "run iteratively during development". Fast feedback loops enable rapid experimentation.

Lesson 5

BTC Dominance Must Be Handled Explicitly

Without BTC residual decomposition, 70-90% of your model is just predicting Bitcoin. Explicitly removing the BTC component forces the model to find coin-specific signals, which is where sustainable alpha lives.

Lesson 6

Automation Compounds Quality

Running the pipeline manually once is feasible. Running it perfectly every hour, every day, for months is not. GitHub Actions automation eliminated human error from data collection, feature computation, and model inference. The system improves every day simply by accumulating more data.

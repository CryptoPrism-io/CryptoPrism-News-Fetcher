Chapter 1

The Big Picture

CryptoPrism is a machine that reads crypto news, crunches price data, and tells you which coins are likely to go up or down over the next few days.

Every hour, it fetches fresh articles from 33 news sources. Every day, it looks at price movements, trading volume, market sentiment, and dozens of technical indicators for 1,000 cryptocurrencies. A machine learning model digests all of this and ranks every coin from "most likely to rise" to "most likely to fall."

The system runs automatically on GitHub's cloud servers. No human intervention needed. The predictions land in a database that trading bots can read from directly.

Chapter 2

The Journey: How We Got Here

The project started as a simple news collector and evolved into a full prediction engine over six months. Here is the story, told in four phases.

Sep 2025

Phase 1 — Collecting the news

We built a program that connects to crypto news websites (CoinDesk, then CryptoCompare) and downloads every article they publish. It runs every hour on GitHub Actions, storing each article's title, body, source, and category in a database. By February 2026 we had accumulated 66,000+ articles from 33 sources. Think of this as building a newspaper archive that updates itself every 60 minutes.

Feb 2026

Phase 2 — Teaching it to read

Raw articles are just text. To make them useful, we added a sentiment AI called FinBERT — a language model trained specifically on financial text. It reads each article and scores it as positive, negative, or neutral. These scores are then grouped by coin and averaged into daily "news signals": Is the news about Bitcoin bullish or bearish today? Is article volume spiking for Ethereum? Are there regulatory stories about Solana? These signals become ingredients the prediction model can learn from.

Apr 8

Phase 3 — The big fix

We discovered the model had been "blind" — a database wiring bug meant it could only see 1 out of 54 available signals during training. It was essentially guessing. The fix was architectural: we connected the training pipeline to a separate historical database (cp_backtest) that holds years of price data, while keeping real-time news on the production database (dbcp). This "dual-database" approach unlocked all 46 price features overnight. Prediction quality jumped from random to genuinely useful.

Apr 9–11

Phase 4 — Making it smarter

With the plumbing fixed, we focused on giving the model better information. We added a BTC decomposition (stripping out Bitcoin's influence to see each coin's own movement), a market regime detector (is the market in "risk on" or "risk off" mode?), neural networks for pattern recognition, and 16 new engineered features measuring momentum ranks and market health. The model went from 34 features to 50 and hit our accuracy target.

Chapter 3

The Three Databases

CryptoPrism uses three separate databases on the same server, each with a different job. Think of them as three filing cabinets, each holding a different type of information.

DATABASE 1

dbcp — The Live Dashboard

The "production" database. Updated every day with fresh news sentiment, fear & greed scores, and the model's latest predictions. This is what the trading bots read from. It only holds recent data — think of it as today's newspaper, not the archive.

DATABASE 2

cp_backtest — The History Book

The "training" database. Contains years of daily price data, technical indicators, and all the engineered features (residuals, cross-coin ranks, etc.) for hundreds of coins. Millions of rows. This is where the model learns patterns from the past.

DATABASE 3

cp_backtest_h — The Microscope

Hourly price data for 250 coins. Used when we need fine-grained detail — like computing the BTC residual decomposition (which needs 30 days of hourly data to work) or training the hourly neural network models.

Why three databases?

The live database (dbcp) only has snapshots — it knows what happened today, not what happened two years ago. The model needs history to learn, so it reads from cp_backtest during training. When we discovered the model was only reading from dbcp (the "blind training" bug), all the historical features were empty. Connecting it to cp_backtest was the single biggest improvement in the entire project.

Chapter 4

Feature Engineering: The FE Tables

"Feature Engineering" (FE) is the process of turning raw data into useful signals the model can learn from. Each "FE table" in the database holds a different family of signals. Here is what each one measures, in plain terms.

Table What it measures Plain English
FE_PCT_CHANGE Daily returns, cumulative return, volatility, risk How much did this coin move today? How risky is it?
FE_MOMENTUM_SIGNALS Rate of change, Williams %R, CMO, SMI Is this coin on a hot streak, or losing steam?
FE_OSCILLATORS_SIGNALS MACD, CCI, ADX, Ultimate Oscillator, Trix Is the coin overbought or oversold? Is a reversal coming?
FE_TVV_SIGNALS On-Balance Volume, SMA/EMA crossovers, CMF Is money flowing into or out of this coin?
FE_RATIOS_SIGNALS Alpha, Beta, Sharpe, Sortino, Win Rate Is this coin giving good returns for the risk you take?
FE_FEAR_GREED_CMC CoinMarketCap Fear & Greed Index Is the overall market feeling greedy or scared today?
FE_NEWS_SIGNALS Sentiment scores, article volume, event flags What is the news saying about this coin? Is coverage spiking?
FE_BTC_RESIDUALS Beta, alpha, residual after stripping BTC movement If Bitcoin moved 5%, how much of this coin's move was just following Bitcoin?
FE_RESIDUAL_FEATURES Momentum, z-score, vol regime, autocorrelation of residuals After removing Bitcoin's influence, is this coin's own movement trending or reversing?
FE_CROSS_COIN Percentile ranks, market breadth, dispersion, HHI How is this coin doing compared to every other coin today?
The key insight

No single table is very predictive on its own. The model's power comes from combining all of them — 50 signals per coin per day. A coin might look great on momentum but terrible on risk-adjusted ratios. Or the news might be bullish but the market breadth says the rally is narrow and fragile. The model weighs all of these trade-offs simultaneously, which is something no human could do across 1,000 coins every day.

The Hourly Pipeline: How Data Flows

The system runs on autopilot via GitHub Actions — cloud servers that execute code on a schedule, like a reliable employee who never sleeps. Here is what happens every day.

Every hour News Fetch Downloads 30–60 new articles from CryptoCompare, stores in database
00:00 CryptoPrism-DB External system updates all FE_* tables with today's prices and indicators
00:30 NLP Pipeline FinBERT reads today's articles, scores sentiment, creates news signals
01:00 ML Signals (daily) Refreshes features, runs model, ranks all 1,000 coins → ML_SIGNALS

All of this runs automatically. The only manual steps are one-time backfill jobs (like populating a new feature table with historical data) and occasional bug fixes.

Chapter 6

The BTC Residual Trick

One of the most important ideas in this system is BTC residual decomposition. Here is the intuition.

In crypto, when Bitcoin goes up 5%, most altcoins go up too — some more, some less. If you are trying to predict which coins will outperform, you first need to strip out this "following Bitcoin" effect. Otherwise your model just learns "buy everything when BTC is up," which is not useful.

The decomposition works like this: for each coin, we run a 30-day rolling regression against Bitcoin. This gives us a beta (how much the coin follows BTC) and a residual (whatever is left over — the coin's own, independent movement).

Example

Say Ethereum went up 8% today and Bitcoin went up 5%. If Ethereum's beta to Bitcoin is 1.2, we'd expect it to go up 6% (1.2 × 5%) just from following BTC. The residual is the extra 2% — that's Ethereum's own alpha. Our model focuses on predicting these residuals, not the raw price moves.

WS6 then takes these hourly residuals and extracts second-order patterns: Is the residual trending? Is it stretched far from its average (likely to snap back)? Is the volatility of residuals expanding (possible breakout)? These become 8 additional features.

Chapter 7

Cross-Coin Features: Comparing Everyone

Most financial features look at a coin in isolation: "Solana went up 5% today." But that number means very different things depending on what the rest of the market did.

Cross-coin features answer: "How is this coin doing compared to every other coin right now?" Instead of raw values, we compute percentile ranks. A coin at the 90th percentile for daily return means it outperformed 90% of all other coins that day.

We also compute market-wide health metrics that are the same for every coin on a given day:

MARKET HEALTH

Breadth

What percentage of coins are above their 20-day moving average? If 80% are above, the rally is broad and healthy. If only 30% are above while the index is rising, it's a narrow rally driven by a few big names — more fragile.

MARKET STRUCTURE

Dispersion

How spread out are the returns? High dispersion means some coins are up big while others are down big. This is actually good for stock-picking models — there is more alpha to capture when coins are moving differently from each other.

Adding these 8 cross-coin features was the single biggest improvement to model stability. It collapsed the gap between validation and test performance, meaning the model generalizes to unseen data rather than memorizing past patterns.

Chapter 8

Results: How Good Are the Predictions?

We measure prediction quality with two main metrics:

IC (Information Coefficient) — how well the model's rankings correlate with what actually happened. An IC of 0 means random guessing. An IC of 0.05 is useful in traditional finance. An IC of 0.10+ is considered strong for daily crypto signals.

Sharpe Ratio — risk-adjusted return. A Sharpe above 2 is very good. Above 5 is exceptional (though may not persist).

Chapter 10

Key Lessons

LESSON 1

Data plumbing matters more than model complexity

The single biggest improvement came from fixing a database routing bug — not from using fancier AI. A simple model with good data beats a complex model with broken data every time.

LESSON 2

Context is everything

A coin going up 5% tells you nothing until you know what the rest of the market did. Cross-coin features (ranks, breadth, dispersion) turned an unstable model into a generalizing one.

LESSON 3

Strip the market, find the alpha

Most crypto movement is just "following Bitcoin." By removing that with BTC residual decomposition, we let the model focus on each coin's unique story — which is where the actual prediction signal lives.

LESSON 4

Automate everything

Ten GitHub Actions workflows run the entire pipeline without human intervention. News fetching, sentiment scoring, feature generation, model retraining, and daily predictions all happen automatically, every day, on schedule.
